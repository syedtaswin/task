Common issues citizens raise

Below are typical categories of public concerns and practical next steps you can take.

Public services & utilities

Problems with water supply, electricity, road maintenance or waste collection that affect neighbourhoods.

What to do
  1. Document the issue with photos, dates, and affected address.
  2. Check your local civic portal for complaint tracking or helpline numbers.
  3. If no response, escalate to local councillor or ombudsman with your documentation.

Corruption & transparency

Concerns about misuse of public funds, bribery or lack of transparency in contracts and decisions.

What to do
  1. Collect any verifiable evidence and note dates, people involved and transactions.
  2. File a complaint with anti-corruption agencies or use RTI/FOI requests where available.
  3. Speak with journalists, CSOs, or public-interest lawyers if the issue is systemic.

Access to healthcare & education

Lack of access to medicines, qualified staff, or poor infrastructure in public hospitals and schools.

What to do
  1. Collect records (receipts, appointment logs) and speak to facility managers.
  2. Contact the district health/education office and submit a written complaint.
  3. Use social media responsibly to highlight urgent problems — include facts and avoid unverified claims.

Safety & law enforcement

Problems such as delayed response, harassment, or police misconduct.

What to do
  1. Prioritize safety — call emergency services if immediate danger exists.
  2. File an FIR/official report; keep copies. Note names, badge numbers, vehicle IDs where possible.
  3. Approach human-rights commissions or legal aid clinics for guidance on escalation.

Environment & public health

Air/water pollution, illegal dumping, and other hazards affecting community health.

What to do
  1. Gather evidence (photos, dates, medical reports if relevant).
  2. Report to pollution control boards or local environmental departments.
  3. Engage local NGOs to monitor and pressure authorities for remedial action.

How to make an effective complaint

  1. Be factual: include dates, locations, names and evidence.
  2. Use official channels first — many governments track and resolve tickets.
  3. Keep copies of every communication and note the complaint/ticket number.
  4. Escalate politely if deadlines pass — refer to earlier ticket numbers and include new evidence.
Sample complaint template 
To: [Department/Official]\nSubject: Complaint regarding [short description]\n\nI am writing to report [detailed description with dates, address, and evidence].\n\nAttachments: [photos, receipts, medical reports]\n\nPlease provide a ticket/reference number and an expected timeline for resolution.\n\nSincerely,\n[Your name] — [Contact number/email]
